The Age of Digital Education

Last year, three professors at Stanford opened their Computer Science courses to the world, expecting participation from a modest number of eager e-learners. Within a couple months, hundreds of thousands had signed up, launching the age of ubiquitous, immersive, adaptive, interactive, lifelong, world class, free online education.


In fact, MIT had been releasing lectures for years, but Stanford truly redefined the 'free Internet course' by enabling peer and teacher interaction, requiring in-class problem solving, regular homework assignments and exams with deadlines, and administering, to the deserving, an official certificate of completion. The experiment proved popular beyond wildest expectation. One class, Artificial Intelligence, attracted 160,000 students from 190 countries. Its inspired professor, Sebastian Thrun, promptly launched an online university Udacity, which delivers AI, and a growing trove of highly interactive online classes, all taught by authorities on the subject.

 

But Udacity has competition from another Stanford professor who opened his Machine Learning class to the world that fall. Andrew Ng founded Coursera, which copies Udacity but has, to date, amassed a wider offering because of intimate collaboration with Stanford, Princeton, UPenn, and University of Michigan. Coursera and Udacity are not lone companies in a barren online education market. There is also iTunes University, MIT Open Courseware (who May 2012 announced 60$ million dollar collaboration with Harvard), Udemy and the Khan Acadamy, to name just the titans. But Coursera and Udacity are the first to deliver a structured, interactive course that nearly rivals the traditional classroom.
